EEOC CHARGE OF DISCRIMINATION — NARRATIVE STATEMENT

(For use in EEOC online intake form — Section: "Describe what happened")

I, Christopher Fowler, was employed by Centene Corporation as a full-time remote employee. I was terminated on or about November 2025. I believe my termination was unlawful under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq., and constituted interference with my rights under the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA), 29 U.S.C. § 2615(a)(1).

My Disability / Serious Health Condition: I have a serious health condition for which I was receiving ongoing medical treatment. This condition caused periodic incapacity and required intermittent absences from work, which I reported to my employer consistent with FMLA and company policy. My condition constitutes a disability under the ADA in that it substantially limits one or more major life activities, and my employer was aware of my condition through the FMLA leave process.

Approved FMLA Leave: On November 3, 2025, I requested intermittent FMLA leave through Centene’s designated leave administrator, Lincoln Financial Group. On November 4, 2025, Lincoln Financial issued a written Intermittent Leave Acknowledgement (Leave ID 18087586) confirming my approved leave from October 24, 2025 through January 1, 2026.

Discriminatory Termination: On November 20, 2025, my supervisor, Victoria Florence, sent me a written communication showing I had accumulated 8.5 attendance points under Centene’s rolling points policy, which has an 8-point termination threshold. Included in that count were two absences that fell within my approved FMLA leave window: November 13, 2025 (0.5 points) and November 18, 2025 (1.0 point), totaling 1.5 FMLA-protected points. Without those protected absences, my total was 7.0 — below the termination threshold. My employer terminated me based on an attendance count that improperly included disability-related, FMLA-protected absences.

Failure to Accommodate: Rather than excluding my FMLA-protected medical absences from the attendance point calculation — a straightforward accommodation required under both the FMLA and ADA — my employer counted those absences as disciplinary violations and used them to justify my termination. My employer was on notice of my approved leave 16 days before compiling the attendance count that triggered termination.

Pattern of Conduct: Prior to the November FMLA violations, in April 2025, Centene’s own Risk Management department remotely locked my work computer for approximately seven days due to personal travel outside the United States. I was unable to work through no fault of my own. During that period, an unauthorized leave of absence was placed on my record without my knowledge or consent. Attendance points were assessed for those involuntary absent days. This earlier incident further supports a pattern of my employer treating my disability-related and involuntary absences as misconduct rather than providing appropriate accommodation or exclusion.

Comparator / Policy Application: I am not aware of non-disabled employees having FMLA-protected or involuntary absences counted against them in the same manner. I believe I was treated differently because of my disability and my use of medical leave.

Relief Requested: I request that the EEOC investigate this charge, make a determination of reasonable cause, and facilitate conciliation. If conciliation fails, I request a Right to Sue letter so that I may pursue my claims in federal court. I seek reinstatement or front pay, back pay from the date of termination, compensatory damages, and any other relief the Commission deems appropriate.
