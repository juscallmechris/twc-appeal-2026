I am writing to file a formal complaint against my former employer, Centene Corporation, for violation of the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA), 29 U.S.C. § 2601 et seq. II. Employment Background

I was employed by Centene Corporation as a full-time remote employee for more than 12 months and worked the required minimum hours to qualify for FMLA coverage. I was subject to Centene’s rolling attendance points system, which imposed discipline at the following thresholds: 3 points = written warning; 6 points = Performance Improvement Plan; 8 points = termination.

III. Approved FMLA Leave

On November 3, 2025, I submitted a request for intermittent FMLA leave to Lincoln Financial Group, Centene’s designated leave administrator. On November 4, 2025, Lincoln Financial issued a written Intermittent Leave Acknowledgement (Leave ID No. 18087586) confirming that my FMLA leave had been received and backdated. The acknowledgement established my approved leave dates as October 24, 2025 through January 1, 2026, for a serious health condition under my own medical care.

IV. The Violation

On November 20, 2025, my supervisor, Victoria Florence (Operations Supervisor, Centene Corporation), sent me a written communication listing my full attendance occurrence history and a total of 8.5 attendance points, which triggered the company’s 8-point termination threshold. Among the occurrences listed were:

  • November 13, 2025 — 0.5 points
  • November 18, 2025 — 1.0 point

Both of these dates fall within my approved FMLA leave period of October 24, 2025 through January 1, 2026. Despite this, Centene included them in my attendance point total. Had these two FMLA-protected absences been properly excluded, my total would have been 7.0 points — below the 8-point termination threshold. I would not have been terminated.

Counting FMLA-protected absences as attendance violations is a well-established form of FMLA interference prohibited by 29 U.S.C. § 2615(a)(1) and the implementing regulations at 29 C.F.R. § 825.220(b). Centene was on notice of my approved leave — Lincoln Financial had issued the formal acknowledgement letter on November 4, 2025, sixteen days before the occurrence email was compiled.

V. Secondary Incident (April 2025)

I also wish to document a related incident from April 2025. Upon returning from personal travel to London on April 15, 2025, Centene’s Risk Management department remotely locked my company-issued laptop because it had been used outside the United States. My supervisor acknowledged in writing that the lockout was caused by Centene’s own security tracking system. I was unable to access any work applications or perform my job duties for approximately seven days (April 16–22, 2025) through no fault of my own. During that period, a leave of absence was placed on my personnel record without my knowledge or consent — a fact I explicitly disputed in writing. Attendance points were nonetheless assessed for those involuntary absent days. While these specific points may have rolled off the 6-month rolling window by the time of termination, the incident demonstrates a broader pattern of attendance consequences being applied to circumstances caused by Centene’s own actions.

VI. Requested Relief

I respectfully request that the Wage and Hour Division:

  • Investigate Centene Corporation’s attendance policies and their application to my absences on November 13 and November 18, 2025
  • Determine whether my termination constituted unlawful interference with my FMLA rights
  • Order appropriate remedies, including back pay for lost wages from the date of termination, liquidated damages as provided under 29 U.S.C. § 2617, and any other equitable relief deemed appropriate
