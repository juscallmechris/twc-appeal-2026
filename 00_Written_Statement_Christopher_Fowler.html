V. The Core FMLA Violation

November 13, 2025 and November 18, 2025 both fall within my approved FMLA leave period of October 24, 2025 through January 1, 2026, as documented in the Lincoln Financial Acknowledgement letter (Leave ID 18087586).

Despite this, Centene counted both of those dates as attendance violations — 0.5 points for November 13 and 1.0 point for November 18 — a combined total of 1.5 points from FMLA-protected absences.

The Math Is Dispositive Total points with Nov 13 + Nov 18 included: 8.5 (above 8-point termination threshold)
Total points with those two dates removed: 7.0 (below 8-point threshold)
Without those two FMLA-protected absences, I would not have been terminated.

Under the FMLA, 29 U.S.C. § 2615(a)(1), it is unlawful for an employer to interfere with, restrain, or deny the exercise of any right provided under the Act. Counting FMLA-protected absences as attendance violations in a points-based termination system constitutes FMLA interference. My employer did exactly that.

I had an active, approved FMLA leave covering both dates. Centene was fully aware of this leave — Lincoln Financial is Centene’s own designated leave administrator, and the acknowledgement letter had been issued 16 days before my supervisor sent the occurrence update email counting those same protected days against me.

I was unable to access any work applications or perform my job duties. I followed up with my supervisor on April 18, 2025, and received no resolution. On April 22, 2025 — seven days after the lockout began — I was contacted by Centene’s Absence Management department, who informed me that my applications had been disabled because a leave of absence had been placed on my account beginning April 15 — a leave I never requested. Access to my systems was restored on April 23, 2025. I returned to work immediately. Despite this, attendance points were assessed for days I was unable to work due solely to a security lockout enforced by Centene’s own Risk Management team, and for which an unauthorized leave of absence was placed on my record without my knowledge or consent.

This incident is relevant because: (1) it resulted in attendance points for an involuntary absence caused by Centene’s own actions; (2) a leave of absence was placed on my record without my knowledge, calling into question the integrity of my leave history; and (3) together with the November FMLA incident, it reflects a pattern of attendance consequences being imposed on me for situations beyond my control.

VIII. Conclusion

My termination from Centene Corporation was not the result of genuine, unexcused attendance misconduct. It resulted from an attendance point total that was artificially inflated by the improper inclusion of two FMLA-protected absences — November 13 and November 18, 2025 — that fell within my approved leave (Leave ID 18087586, October 24, 2025 through January 1, 2026). Without those two protected absences, my total was 7.0 points — below the 8-point termination threshold. I was terminated because my employer failed to exclude FMLA-protected time from its attendance calculations, in direct violation of federal law. That same violation deprives me of unemployment benefits I am rightfully entitled to receive.

I respectfully request that the Commission reverse the prior decision and find that I am eligible to receive unemployment benefits.
